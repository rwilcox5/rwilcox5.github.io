Why are some parlays better than others?

The NFL is interconnected enough that it is impossible to judge the quality of any team without assuming information about the rest of the league. In particular when two teams play each other it is somewhat easy to determine which team is better, but it is much harder to determine whether both teams are good or both teams are bad. Thus their rankings will be intertwined. If one of these teams is later upset then it is more likely that the other team is worse than expected as well. Hence betting on a parlay with both teams can provide better odds than two individual bets.

Consider a league with 4 teams: Arizona, Baltimore, Chicago, and Detroit. There are 4 choices for which team is the best, and then 3 choices for second best, and then 2 choices for third best, and then there is only 1 choice left for the worst team. Thus there are 24 possible rankings of these 4 teams. Arizona is better than Baltimore is better than Chicago is better than Detroit, or Detroit is better than Chicago is better than Baltimore is better than Arizona, or any of the other 22 similar rankings. It is an expansion league and we have no information about the teams so each of these rankings is equally likely.

Pretend that the league removes all randomness so the better team always wins. In week 1, Arizona beats Baltimore and Chicago beats Detroit. We want to bet on week 2 games. Now we are ready to place some bets. The probability that Arizona beats Chicago is 50%. Thus a sportsbook should put the odds of Arizona winning around -110. So placing a $110 bet on Arizona would pay out $210 if they win. The other game is also a tossup so every team should have odds of -110. The sportsbook will take their cut, but picking the correct team should almost double your bet.

The way a sportsbook computes parlay odds is to multiply the odds of each outcome. If the odds of picking each game correctly are 50% then the odds of picking both games will be 25%. A sportsbook that doesn't take a cut would place the parlay odds at +300 so a $100 bet would payout $400 if both teams win. In reality the odds are generally +260 so the payout is $360.

So what are the actual probabilities of a parlay paying out? We can look at our table and see which rows payout for which bets. The last 4 columns are the outcomes of each parlay. For example the A and B column is the outcome of a parlay on Arizona and Baltimore to win.

The Arizona and Baltimore parlay wins 2 out of 6 times. Each of the 6 rankings is equally likely with the information from week 1 so this parlay will payout 1/3 of the time. Since we get $360 for each win our expected return is $360/3 which is $120. Our bet was only $100 so we actually have a positive expected return from the parlay. We knew no more information than the sportsbook about the outcomes, but were able to get an edge. Betting on Arizona and Detroit, however, would be a terrible bet as we only win 1/6 of the time for an expected payout of $60 on a $100 bet. Of the four parlays, betting on Arizona and Baltimore to win and also betting the opposite that Chicago and Detroit win would mean one of the parlays would payout 2/3 of the time. Thus $200 would become $360 with probability 2/3.

So there are theoretical scenarios where beating the sportsbook is possible without knowing anything extra about the teams. All of the above make good exercises for a combinatorics or proabability classroom, but what about in real life? First of all, there is too much randomness to eliminate any possible rankings. But maybe we can still learn enough to get an edge on the sportsbook.

Let's redo the above example, but assume that sometimes the superior team loses to an inferior foe. We will use ELO ratings to make probabilities easy to compute. We have the same 4 teams that we know nothing about except that they have different abilities. The best team has an ELO rating of 1575, the second best a rating of 1525, the third best a rating of 1475, and the worst has a rating of 1425. If you don't care to learn more about ELO ratings then all you need to know is that the best team has a 57.1% chance of beating the second best team, a 64.0% chance of beating the third best, and a 70.3% chance of beating the worst team. Similarly, the second best team has a 57.1% chance of beating the third best team and a 64.0% chance of beating the worst team. The third best team has a 57.1% chance of beating the worst team. We still do not know which teams are better than other teams, and so we need to determine the probability of each possible ranking.

Before week 1 we know nothing about the teams so each of the 24 possible rankings is equally likely to occur. But what happens after Arizona beats Baltimore in week 1. We cannot eliminate the possibility that Baltimore is better than Arizona, but we do want to make consider those rankings as less likely to occur since Arizona is probably better than Baltimore. Given that Arizona beat Baltimore, what is the probability that Arizona is better than Baltimore?

We need to know a little bit about conditional probabilities. P(A|B) is notation for the probability that event A happens given that event B happened. We know that B happened so if A happens that means that A and B occured. So we want the probability of A and B occuring. But we only care about situations where B happened so we divide the probability of both by the probability that B happened. Thus P(A|B)=P(A and B)/P(B). Simlarly, P(B|A)=P(A and B)/P(A) so multiplying by P(A) we get that P(A and B)=P(B|A)*P(A). We can plug this equality into the previous to yield P(A|B)=P(B|A)*P(A)/P(B).

So how does P(A|B)=P(B|A)*P(A)/P(B) relate to our scenario. We want to probability that Arizona is better than Baltimore given that Arizona beat Baltimore. Thus we want to compute P(Arizona better than Baltimore | Arizona beat Baltimore). We know this probability will equal P(Arizona beat Baltimore | Arizona better than Baltimore)*P(Arizona is better than Baltimore)/P(Arizona beat Baltimore).

Unfortunately it does matter whether Arizona is a lot better than Baltimore or just a little bit better so we will break up our question into three parts. First, what is the probability that Arizona is 1 position better than Baltimore? That is, what is the probability that Arizona is best while Baltimore is second best or Arizona is second best and Baltimore third best or Arizona is third best and Baltimore is the worst? We want to compute P(Arizona beat Baltimore | Arizona 1 spot better than Baltimore)*P(Arizona is 1 spot better than Baltimore)/P(Arizona beat Baltimore).

When we add up all of the probabilities we get the probability of Arizona beating Chicago to be 50%. That makes sense because there is really no reason to expect either team to be better than the other based on what we know. And the same is true for Baltimore beating Detroit. The expected parlay odds for Arizona and Baltimore winning without a cut for the sportsbook would be +300 again. What are the computed odds that Arizona and Baltimore both win? 25.05%. So these two events are not independent, but you would need to find a very generous bookie to make this edge profitable.

The good news is that there an edge exploit. We just need to hope that there is some way to magnify that advantage by a large multiple. We are basing our strategy on two games so the randomness is bound to have too much of an impact. After several more games the advantage should increase, but then the computations become more involved. Trying to rank all 32 NFL teams with this method requires computing 32! probabilities so we will need to simplify the process.

Maybe World Cup parlays are the sweet spot for this theory.
